General Dietary Guidance

Evidence-based nutrition principles for cancer patients

Food Safety First

  • Wash hands frequently with antibacterial soap
  • Cook foods to safe internal temperatures
  • Avoid raw or undercooked foods during treatment
  • Store foods properly and check expiration dates
  • Use separate cutting boards for raw and cooked foods

Stay Hydrated

  • Aim for 8-10 glasses of water daily minimum
  • Include herbal teas, broths, and electrolyte drinks
  • Limit caffeine and avoid alcohol during treatment
  • Monitor urine color as hydration indicator
  • Sip fluids throughout the day, not just at meals

Protein Power

  • Include high-quality protein at every meal
  • Choose lean sources: fish, poultry, eggs, legumes
  • Consider protein supplements if needed
  • Aim for 1.2-1.6g per kg body weight daily
  • Combine plant proteins for complete amino acids

Nutritious Choices

  • Eat a rainbow of colorful fruits and vegetables
  • Choose whole grains over refined options
  • Include healthy fats: avocado, nuts, olive oil
  • Consider small, frequent meals (5-6 per day)
  • Focus on nutrient-dense, anti-inflammatory foods

Learn About Cancer Nutrition

Educational content to help you understand nutrition during cancer treatment

Nutrition Fundamentals

Protein During Treatment

Protein needs increase during cancer treatment to help repair tissues, maintain immune function, and prevent muscle loss. Aim for 1.2-1.6g per kg of body weight.

Good Sources: Lean meats, fish, eggs, beans, dairy products, nuts, quinoa, soy products
Based on NCI Protein Requirements Guidelines 2024

Calories & Energy

Maintaining adequate calorie intake prevents unwanted weight loss and provides energy for healing and daily activities. Cancer patients may need 25-35 calories per kg body weight.

Energy-Dense Foods: Nuts, avocados, olive oil, smoothies, dried fruits, whole grain cereals
ACS Nutrition Guidelines for Cancer Survivors

Hydration Importance

Proper hydration helps manage side effects, supports kidney function, aids in drug metabolism, and helps prevent constipation during treatment.

Hydration Tips: Water, herbal teas, broths, water-rich fruits and vegetables, diluted fruit juices
WHO Hydration Guidelines for Cancer Patients

Micronutrients & Vitamins

Vitamins and minerals play crucial roles in immune function, wound healing, and energy metabolism during cancer treatment.

Key Nutrients: Vitamin D, B-complex, Vitamin C, zinc, selenium, folate
WHO Micronutrient Requirements for Cancer Care

Fiber & Digestive Health

Adequate fiber intake supports digestive health, helps maintain gut microbiome, and may reduce treatment-related constipation.

Fiber Sources: Whole grains, legumes, fruits with skin, vegetables, nuts and seeds
Journal of Clinical Oncology 2024

Antioxidants & Immune Support

Antioxidants help protect cells from damage and support immune function. Focus on food sources rather than high-dose supplements.

Antioxidant Foods: Berries, colorful vegetables, green tea, dark leafy greens, tomatoes
Nutrition and Cancer Journal 2024

Managing Treatment Side Effects

Nausea & Vomiting

Small, frequent meals work better than large ones. Cold or room temperature foods may be better tolerated than hot foods. Ginger has clinically proven anti-nausea properties.

Helpful Foods: Ginger tea, crackers, toast, clear broths, peppermint, bland starches
NCI Managing Nausea Clinical Guidelines

Mouth Sores & Mucositis

Choose soft, moist foods and avoid acidic, spicy, or rough-textured foods. Maintain oral hygiene and consider nutritional supplements if eating is severely impacted.

Gentle Options: Smoothies, yogurt, mashed fruits, soups, ice cream, protein shakes
Supportive Care in Cancer Journal

Changes in Taste & Smell

Treatment can alter taste perception (dysgeusia) and smell (anosmia). Experiment with herbs, spices, and temperature to enhance flavors and maintain nutrition.

Flavor Enhancers: Lemon, herbs, marinades, different temperatures, strong spices
Oncology Nutrition Connection

Fatigue & Low Energy

Cancer-related fatigue affects eating patterns. Plan meals and snacks when energy levels are highest, usually mornings for many patients.

Energy Strategies: Meal prep, ready-to-eat snacks, liquid nutrition, small frequent meals
ACS Nutrition Guidelines

Appetite Changes

Both appetite loss and increased appetite can occur. Focus on nutrient-dense foods and work with your healthcare team to manage changes.

Appetite Support: Regular meal schedule, pleasant eating environment, social dining
NCI Eating Hints Guidelines

Diarrhea & Digestive Issues

Some treatments cause digestive upset. BRAT diet (Bananas, Rice, Applesauce, Toast) can help, along with probiotics and adequate fluid replacement.

Gentle Foods: BRAT foods, probiotics, electrolyte solutions, cooked vegetables
WHO Food Safety Guidelines

Nutrition by Treatment Type

Chemotherapy Nutrition

Focus on maintaining weight and managing side effects. Eat before treatment when appetite is usually better. Immune system may be compromised, requiring food safety precautions.

Key Points: Food safety protocols, nausea management, maintain hydration, nutrient timing
NCI Nutrition in Cancer Care PDQ 2024

Radiation Therapy

Nutrition needs vary by treatment area. GI radiation may cause different side effects than head/neck radiation. Skin care and adequate nutrition support healing.

Considerations: Location-specific side effects, skin care nutrition, hydration, energy needs
ACS Treatment-Specific Nutrition Guide

Targeted & Immunotherapy

Side effects vary by specific medication. Some may cause diarrhea, others skin changes affecting eating. Work with your team for personalized guidance.

Approach: Medication-specific nutrition guidance, symptom management, timing considerations
Journal of Clinical Oncology 2024

Surgery & Recovery

Pre-operative nutrition status affects recovery. Post-surgery, focus on protein for wound healing and gradual return to normal eating patterns.

Surgery Focus: Pre-op optimization, post-op protein needs, gradual diet progression
Supportive Care in Cancer

Bone Marrow Transplant

Requires strict food safety protocols due to severe immunosuppression. Nutrition support is critical for engraftment and recovery.

BMT Nutrition: Neutropenic diet, sterile food preparation, nutrient density focus
WHO Food Safety for Immunocompromised

Supportive Care

Palliative and supportive care focus on comfort and quality of life. Nutrition goals may shift to maintaining enjoyment of food and comfort.

Comfort Care: Quality of life focus, food enjoyment, symptom management, family involvement
Oncology Nutrition Connection

Food Safety for Cancer Patients

Safe Food Temperatures

Proper cooking temperatures kill harmful bacteria. Use food thermometers and avoid undercooked foods, especially during immunosuppression.

Temperature Guide: Poultry 165°F, Ground meat 160°F, Whole cuts 145°F, Seafood 145°F
ACS Food Safety Guidelines 2024

Hygiene & Hand Washing

Proper hand hygiene and clean food preparation surfaces are critical for preventing foodborne illness in immunocompromised patients.

Safety Steps: Wash hands 20 seconds, clean surfaces, separate raw/cooked foods, wash produce
WHO Food Safety for Immunocompromised

Foods to Avoid

During immunosuppression, certain high-risk foods should be avoided to prevent foodborne infections that could be life-threatening.

Avoid: Raw/undercooked meat, unpasteurized dairy, raw sprouts, unwashed produce, deli meats
NCI Food Safety for Cancer Patients

Safe Food Shopping

Choose reputable stores, check expiration dates, maintain cold chain, and shop for perishables last to maintain food quality and safety.

Shopping Tips: Check dates, cold foods last, separate raw meats, sanitize cart handles
CDC Food Safety Recommendations

Proper Food Storage

Correct storage temperatures and timing prevent bacterial growth. Follow first-in-first-out principles and monitor refrigerator temperatures.

Storage Guide: Refrigerator ≤40°F, Freezer 0°F, Use leftovers within 3-4 days
FDA Food Storage Guidelines

Restaurant & Takeout Safety

When eating out, choose reputable establishments, avoid buffets, and ensure hot foods are hot and cold foods are cold upon delivery.

Dining Out: Choose busy restaurants, avoid buffets, check food temperature, ask about ingredients
ACS Safe Dining Guidelines

Global Nutrition Perspectives

Mediterranean Diet Patterns

Rich in antioxidants, healthy fats, and anti-inflammatory compounds. May support cancer recovery and overall health outcomes.

Key Components: Olive oil, fish, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, moderate red wine
Mediterranean Diet and Cancer Research 2024

Asian Dietary Patterns

Traditional Asian diets emphasize plant foods, soy products, green tea, and fish. These patterns are associated with lower cancer rates in some populations.

Asian Elements: Soy foods, green tea, vegetables, rice, fish, minimal processed foods
Asian Diet Patterns and Cancer Prevention

Plant-Based Approaches

Emphasis on plant foods may provide protective compounds and fiber. Can be adapted globally while respecting cultural food preferences.

Plant Focus: Vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, limited animal products
Plant-Based Nutrition in the Americas

Regional Food Access

Nutrition recommendations must consider regional food availability, cultural practices, and economic factors affecting food access globally.

Access Considerations: Local foods, seasonal availability, cultural preferences, economic factors
WHO Regional Food Access Study

Cultural Dietary Considerations

Cancer nutrition care must respect cultural food practices, religious dietary laws, and traditional healing approaches while ensuring nutritional adequacy.

Cultural Aspects: Religious practices, traditional foods, family involvement, cooking methods
ACS Cultural Dietary Considerations

WHO Global Nutrition Targets

International guidelines focus on reducing malnutrition, improving micronutrient status, and addressing nutrition-related chronic diseases including cancer.

Global Goals: Reduce malnutrition, improve micronutrient status, prevent chronic diseases
WHO Global Nutrition Targets 2025

Separating Myths from Facts

Myth

"Sugar feeds cancer, so avoid all carbohydrates"

Fact

All cells use glucose for energy. Complex carbohydrates provide sustained energy and important nutrients needed during treatment. Focus on nutrient-dense carbs.

Myth

"Supplements can replace a healthy diet"

Fact

Whole foods provide nutrients in forms that are better absorbed and utilized. Supplements should only be used under medical supervision to address specific deficiencies.

Myth

"Organic foods prevent cancer recurrence"

Fact

While organic foods may reduce pesticide exposure, there's no evidence they prevent recurrence. Focus on eating a variety of fruits and vegetables, organic or conventional.

Myth

"Alkaline diets cure cancer by changing body pH"

Fact

The body tightly regulates pH levels. Diet cannot significantly change blood pH. Focus on balanced nutrition rather than trying to manipulate body chemistry.

Myth

"Antioxidant supplements boost immunity during treatment"

Fact

High-dose antioxidant supplements may interfere with certain cancer treatments. Get antioxidants from food sources and consult your oncology team about supplements.

Myth

"Fasting during treatment helps kill cancer cells"

Fact

Cancer patients need adequate nutrition to support their immune system and healing. Fasting can lead to malnutrition and worsen treatment outcomes.

Myth

"Dairy products cause cancer to spread"

Fact

There's no scientific evidence that dairy causes cancer to spread. Dairy products provide important protein, calcium, and vitamins during treatment.

Myth

"Herbal remedies are safe because they're natural"

Fact

Natural doesn't always mean safe. Many herbs can interact with cancer treatments or cause side effects. Always discuss herbal products with your healthcare team.
